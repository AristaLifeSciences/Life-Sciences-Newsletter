    Always-On Networks: The New Standard for Life Sciences¶

    For research institutions, it can delay critical experiments.

    In life sciences, downtime rarely stays isolated. It cascades across operations, affecting production, research, and GxP compliance, all of which require continuous validation.

    The cost extends beyond IT - lost productivity, delayed therapies, financial impact, and reduced agility. As organizations adopt AI, automation, connected labs, and digital manufacturing, the network becomes the foundation of operations, not just supporting infrastructure.

    Yet many networks still assume change requires disruption: maintenance windows, outages, scheduled upgrades.

    These are now barriers to resilience, compliance, and innovation.

    Leading organizations are moving to a new model: networks that stay available while continuously evolving.

    At Arista, we call this an always-on network. Implementing an always-on network eliminates downtime, strengthens continuous compliance, and enables modernization without disrupting critical scientific and operational workflows.

    When Security, Change, and Uptime Collide¶

    For infrastructure teams, maintaining continuous availability has become increasingly difficult.

    New vulnerabilities are discovered every day, especially now with the rise of Mythos. Software updates are released constantly. Regulatory requirements continue to evolve. At the same time, production environments, laboratory systems, and business-critical applications cannot afford unexpected interruptions.

    This creates a familiar tension across life sciences organizations: Security teams are expected to remediate vulnerabilities quickly. Operations teams are focused on maintaining stability and uptime. Compliance requirements often demand extensive validation and documentation before changes can be introduced.

    As a result, critical updates are frequently delayed while organizations wait for approved maintenance windows or production shutdowns.

    In many environments, exposure is accepted as the price of operational continuity.

    But that tradeoff becomes increasingly difficult to sustain as cyber threats grow more sophisticated and organizations become more dependent on connected infrastructure.

    Always-On Manufacturing: Ensuring Operational Continuity¶

    In pharmaceutical manufacturing, network upgrades are rarely simple infrastructure projects. They occur within highly structured operational environments built around the Purdue Model, where business systems, manufacturing execution systems, industrial control systems, and production assets operate across interconnected network layers.

    Changes made at one layer can have downstream implications for validated applications, production workflows, and critical manufacturing processes. As a result, infrastructure upgrades are often tightly aligned to GxP validation requirements, production schedules, and planned maintenance windows.

    This creates a growing challenge. Vulnerabilities are discovered in days, while remediation opportunities may take months.

    Every delayed patch extends the organization's exposure window. Yet taking systems offline to address security risks can introduce operational risk, disrupt production, and trigger extensive validation efforts.

    For years, manufacturers have been forced to choose between security and uptime.

    Arista changes that equation.

    Built on a fully stateful, modular architecture, Arista EOS enables software components to be upgraded in-service while maintaining operational continuity. Rather than treating upgrades as disruptive events that ripple across the manufacturing environment, EOS isolates change and minimizes operational impact.

    Combined with Smart System Upgrade (SSU), organizations can move away from large-scale, maintenance-window-driven upgrades toward a more continuous and controlled operational model.

    Security updates can be applied when risk dictates, not when production schedules permit. Infrastructure teams can reduce exposure without disrupting manufacturing operations, helping align cybersecurity objectives with the realities of Purdue-based industrial environments and ensuring long-term regulatory compliance.

    Always-On Wireless: Driving Agility and Workforce Continuity¶

    Availability is no longer limited to the wired network.

    Across life sciences organizations, wireless connectivity has become an essential operational service.

    Researchers rely on connected devices throughout laboratories and research facilities. Warehouse personnel depend on mobile scanners and handheld devices to manage inventory and logistics workflows. Manufacturing teams increasingly utilize wireless sensors, robotics, and industrial IoT platforms to drive efficiency and automation.

    When wireless connectivity fails, productivity suffers immediately.

    A dropped connection can interrupt workflows, delay operations, and create visibility gaps that ripple across the organization. As mobility becomes more deeply embedded into day-to-day operations, wireless resilience becomes just as important as wired resiliency.

    Organizations, therefore, need more than wireless coverage. They need wireless infrastructure designed for continuous operation.

    That means seamless roaming, high availability, simplified management, and consistent user experiences across laboratories, offices, manufacturing facilities, and distribution environments.

    An always-on strategy must extend from the data center to the campus edge, ensuring users, devices, and applications remain connected regardless of location.

    The Future Is Always On¶

    For decades, infrastructure teams have planned around downtime.

    Maintenance windows. Scheduled outages. Upgrade cycles.

    The assumption has always been the same: if the network needs to change, the business must accommodate it.

    But that assumption no longer aligns with the realities of modern life sciences.

    As AI, automation, connected manufacturing, and digital research environments continue to expand, the cost of waiting grows higher, and the organizations that can adapt fastest will have a measurable advantage.

    The technology to eliminate downtime-driven operations already exists.

    The real question is: how much longer can organizations afford to operate as though it doesn't?
